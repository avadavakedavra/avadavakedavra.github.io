Kerala HSE Plus Two
Complete Study Plan 2025–26

A month-by-month study roadmap for Plus Two Science stream students — covering Physics, Chemistry, Biology and Mathematics, with chapter priorities, daily schedules, revision strategy, and exam-day guidance tailored specifically to the Kerala DHSE board pattern.

Physics Chemistry Biology Mathematics NEET Ready
By SEHR Academy · Vadanappally, Thrissur · Updated June 2026

Plus Two is the most consequential year of school education in Kerala — your HSE marks determine college admission, NEET and JEE eligibility, and scholarship access. But the year is also ten months long, and most students spend the first six months without a real plan, then scramble in the final four. This guide gives you a structured, subject-wise study plan from June to March, so every month of the academic year is used deliberately, not lost to confusion.

Kerala DHSE Plus Two — Exam Pattern

The Kerala Board of Higher Secondary Education (DHSE) conducts the Plus Two examinations every March. The exam follows a continuous and comprehensive evaluation (CCE) pattern for internal marks, combined with a year-end written board examination.

The theory paper for Science subjects (Physics, Chemistry, Biology) carries 60 marks, with 20 marks from practicals and 20 marks from Continuous Evaluation (CE) — giving a total of 100 marks per subject. Mathematics carries 80 marks theory + 20 marks CE. English Core carries 80 marks theory + 20 marks CE.

Continuous Evaluation (CE) — your easiest 20 marks: CE marks are awarded by your school through unit tests, project work, assignments and practicals. These are entirely predictable. A student who takes every unit test seriously and submits all projects on time can secure 17–20 CE marks per subject — that is 100–120 bonus marks across five subjects before the board exam even begins.

Month-by-Month Study Plan

The Kerala HSE academic year runs from June to March, giving you approximately ten months before board exams. This plan divides that time into four clear phases — Foundation, Core Chapter Study, Deep Revision, and Final Sprint — each with specific subject targets.

Phase 1 — Foundation & Orientation
June
Week 1–4
Foundation
⚡
Physics
Review Class 11 concepts (Mechanics, Waves, Thermodynamics). These form the bedrock for Electric Charges (Force, fields) and Modern Physics (nuclear energy). Do not skip this — weak Class 11 foundations cause avoidable mistakes in Class 12 numericals.
🧪
Chemistry
Revise Class 11 Equilibrium, Thermodynamics, Atomic Structure and Periodic Table. These directly feed into Electrochemistry, Chemical Kinetics and Inorganic Chemistry in Class 12.
🧬
Biology
Review Class 11 Cell Biology, Plant Physiology and Animal Kingdom. Begin Chapter 1 — Reproduction in Organisms. Biology rewards early starters since it has 16 chapters of dense content.
∫
Mathematics
Review Class 11 Functions, Trigonometry and Sets. Begin Chapter 1 — Relations and Functions (Class 12). Practice basic function types and composition from the first week.
Set up subject notebooks with chapter lists — track what you cover weekly.
Collect DHSE previous year question papers (last 5 years) in June itself.
July
Week 5–8
Foundation
⚡
Physics
Chapter 1 — Electric Charges and Fields (Coulomb's law, Gauss's theorem, dipoles) + Chapter 2 — Electrostatic Potential and Capacitance. These are the two highest-scoring chapters. Solve at least 20 numericals from each.
🧪
Chemistry
Chapter 1 — Solutions (colligative properties, Raoult's law, Van't Hoff factor). Start Chapter 2 — Electrochemistry (Nernst equation, cell notation, conductance).
🧬
Biology
Chapter 2 — Sexual Reproduction in Flowering Plants (pollination, fertilisation, endosperm). Chapter 3 — Human Reproduction. These two chapters together account for a significant share of the Reproduction unit.
∫
Mathematics
Chapter 2 — Inverse Trigonometric Functions. Chapter 3 — Matrices. Begin Chapter 4 — Determinants. Maths requires daily practice — even 30 minutes a day at this stage builds the speed needed later.
Phase 2 — Core Chapter Study
August
Week 9–12
Core Chapters
⚡
Physics
Chapter 3 — Current Electricity (Ohm's law, Kirchhoff's laws, potentiometer, metre bridge). Chapter 4 — Moving Charges and Magnetism. These chapters consistently have numericals worth 3–5 marks each in DHSE papers.
🧪
Chemistry
Complete Chapter 2 — Electrochemistry. Chapter 3 — Chemical Kinetics (rate law, Arrhenius equation, integrated rate equations). This is a heavily numerical chapter — practise graph-based problems on reaction order.
🧬
Biology
Chapter 5 — Principles of Inheritance and Variation (Mendel's laws, incomplete dominance, linkage, chromosomal disorders). This is the single most important Biology chapter for both HSE and NEET — spend at least 2 weeks here.
∫
Mathematics
Complete Chapter 4 — Determinants. Begin Chapter 5 — Continuity and Differentiability. This starts the Calculus unit — the most important unit in Class 12 Maths, carrying the largest chunk of marks.
First unit test is usually in August — use it as a checkpoint for Chapters 1–2 across all subjects.
Begin your Biology diagram notebook this month — one clean labelled diagram per chapter, redrawn from memory.
September
Week 13–16
Core Chapters
⚡
Physics
Chapter 5 — Magnetism and Matter. Chapter 6 — Electromagnetic Induction. Chapter 7 — Alternating Current (RMS, phasors, LCR resonance, transformers). Dedicate extra time to AC circuits — they are a consistent source of long-answer questions.
🧪
Chemistry
Chapter 4 — d- and f-Block Elements (transition metals, oxidation states, interstitial compounds). Chapter 5 — Coordination Compounds (Werner's theory, IUPAC nomenclature, isomerism). These are high-memorisation inorganic chapters — use colour-coded notes.
🧬
Biology
Chapter 6 — Molecular Basis of Inheritance (DNA structure, replication, transcription, translation, genetic code). Along with Chapter 5, this forms the Genetics unit — 18 marks in the exam. Practice writing the steps of replication and transcription in sequence.
∫
Mathematics
Chapter 6 — Applications of Derivatives (increasing/decreasing, maxima and minima, tangents). This is the highest marks chapter in Maths — 10 marks in CBSE and equally weighted in Kerala HSE. Solve 40+ problems here before moving on.
October
Week 17–20 · Mid-term exams
Core + First Revision
⚡
Physics
Chapter 8 — EM Waves (short chapter — complete in 2 days). Chapter 9 — Ray Optics (mirrors, lenses, prism, optical instruments — 8 marks). Begin first revision of Chapters 1–8 before mid-term exams.
🧪
Chemistry
Chapter 6 — Haloalkanes and Haloarenes (SN1, SN2, elimination, naming). Begin revision of Chapters 1–5. The Onam holidays in October are an ideal window for a 5-day intensive revision block.
🧬
Biology
Chapter 7 — Evolution (origin of life, Hardy-Weinberg). Chapter 8 — Human Health and Disease. Revise Chapters 1–7 (Reproduction + Genetics + Evolution) — this is 32 of the 60 theory marks.
∫
Mathematics
Chapter 7 — Integrals (substitution, by parts, partial fractions, definite integrals). Integration is time-consuming — do at least 50 problems of varied types this month. Also complete Chapter 8 — Applications of Integrals (area under curves).
October mid-term exams are your first real performance benchmark — review where you lost marks and fix those chapters in November.
Use the Oona/Onam holidays for an intensive 5-day revision of your two weakest subjects.
November
Week 21–24
Core Chapters
⚡
Physics
Chapter 10 — Wave Optics (Huygens' principle, interference, Young's double-slit experiment, diffraction, polarisation). Chapter 11 — Dual Nature of Radiation (photoelectric effect, de Broglie). These chapters are theory-heavy but also carry numericals.
🧪
Chemistry
Chapter 7 — Alcohols, Phenols and Ethers. Chapter 8 — Aldehydes, Ketones and Carboxylic Acids (the highest-marks organic chapter). Focus intensely on name reactions — Aldol condensation, Cannizzaro, Clemmensen, Wolf-Kishner, esterification.
🧬
Biology
Chapter 9 — Microbes in Human Welfare. Chapter 10 — Biotechnology: Principles and Processes (recombinant DNA, restriction enzymes, PCR, gel electrophoresis). These are process-heavy chapters — draw the steps as flowcharts.
∫
Mathematics
Chapter 9 — Differential Equations (variable separable, homogeneous, linear). Chapter 10 — Vector Algebra (dot product, cross product, triple product). Vectors are highly application-based — practise geometrical interpretation problems.
December
Week 25–28
Complete Syllabus
⚡
Physics
Chapter 12 — Atoms. Chapter 13 — Nuclei (binding energy, radioactivity, nuclear reactions). Chapter 14 — Semiconductor Electronics (p-n junction, transistor, logic gates). Entire Physics syllabus must be complete by end of December.
🧪
Chemistry
Chapter 9 — Amines (basicity, diazonium salts, coupling reactions). Chapter 10 — Biomolecules (carbohydrates, proteins, vitamins, nucleic acids). Full Chemistry syllabus complete by 31 December. Begin compiling your name-reaction master list.
🧬
Biology
Chapter 11 — Biotechnology Applications (Bt crops, gene therapy, biopiracy). Chapter 13–16 — Ecology (Organisms & Populations, Ecosystem, Biodiversity, Environmental Issues). All 16 Biology chapters done by December 31.
∫
Mathematics
Chapter 11 — 3D Geometry (direction cosines, lines and planes, shortest distance). Chapter 12 — Linear Programming. Chapter 13 — Probability (Bayes' theorem, binomial distribution). Full Maths syllabus complete by December 31.
Goal: All subjects fully taught by 31 December. Students who finish by this date have all of January and February for revision and mock tests — this is the single biggest advantage you can give yourself.
Phase 3 — Deep Revision & Mock Tests
January
Week 29–32 · Model exams
Deep Revision
⚡
Physics — Full Revision Round 1
Revise all 14 chapters in order. Solve the DHSE model exam paper under timed conditions (3 hours). Check your answers against the marking scheme — identify which mark ranges you are dropping marks in (1-mark, 3-mark or 5-mark questions).
🧪
Chemistry — Name Reactions & Formula Drill
Revise all name reactions (compile one consolidated list — minimum 20 reactions). Revise all electrochemical cell problems and conductance numericals. Solve previous year Chemistry papers (2020–2025) focusing on identifying pattern questions.
🧬
Biology — Diagram Revision
Redraw all key diagrams from memory: T.S. of ovule, DNA replication fork, lac operon, nitrogen cycle, ecosystem energy pyramid, renal system. Diagrams appear in 3-mark and 5-mark questions and carry explicit marks for labelling.
∫
Mathematics — Full Paper Practice
Solve one full 80-mark paper per week under exam conditions. Prioritise Calculus problems — every mark in Applications of Derivatives and Integrals that you can secure reliably is worth 10× the time spent on Linear Programming (5 marks).
The DHSE model examination is typically held in January — treat it exactly like the real board exam. Do not leave the hall early. Review your results within 48 hours.
After model exams, rank your subjects from weakest to strongest. Give your two weakest subjects 50% more daily time in February.
February
Week 33–36 · Practicals
Mock & Practical
⚡
Physics — Practicals + Numerical Drill
Board practicals for Physics are usually held in February or early March. Practise these experiments: verification of Ohm's law, potentiometer experiments, refractive index using glass slab, p-n junction characteristics. Also practise writing observation tables and result statements cleanly.
🧪
Chemistry — Practical + Salt Analysis
Key Chemistry practicals: titration (acid-base, redox), salt analysis (qualitative analysis of cations and anions), chromatography. The salt analysis viva is where most students lose practical marks — practise identifying at least 20 different cation/anion combinations.
🧬
Biology — Practical + Revision Round 2
Biology practicals: pollen germination, onion root tip mitosis, study of epithelial tissue slides, heart rate measurement. Second full theory revision round — focus on chapters where you scored low in model exams.
∫
Mathematics — Chapter-Wise Mock
Solve chapter-specific question banks for your 3 weakest Maths chapters. Solve two full papers in timed conditions. Focus on answer presentation — in DHSE Maths, step-marking is generous but only if working is shown clearly.
Phase 4 — Final Sprint
March — Board Exams
Week 37–40
Final Sprint
📋
3–4 days before each paper
Do one quick full-syllabus skim — not re-reading but scanning your key notes, formula sheets and diagram notebooks. Identify your "safe" topics (where you always score) vs "risky" topics — give risky topics 60% of your time.
📋
Night before the exam
Review only: formula cards, name reactions list, diagram notebook. No new topics. Sleep by 10 PM — a rested brain retrieves information faster than a tired one that crammed until 2 AM.
📋
In the exam hall
Read the question paper fully in the first 15 minutes. Attempt questions you know best first. In Maths, always show all steps — step marks are awarded even for incomplete solutions. In Biology, always include a diagram for 3-mark and 5-mark questions even if not explicitly asked.

Final Month Revision Checklist

In the four weeks before your first board paper, use this checklist to confirm you have covered every important preparation area:

Physics: Solved minimum 3 full DHSE previous year papers under timed conditions
Chemistry: Name-reaction master list compiled and revised at least 3 times from memory
Biology: All 15+ key diagrams drawn from memory and labels checked against textbook
Mathematics: Solved at least 5 full 80-mark papers in timed conditions, step-marking reviewed
All subjects: DHSE model question paper 2025–26 solved and answers compared with the official key
Practicals: All experiment procedures memorised; observation tables practised; record book neat and complete
CE marks: All assignments and projects submitted; periodic test scores confirmed with teachers
Formula cards: One-page formula sheet made for each Science subject — verified and accurate
English: Letter writing, note-making, and literature essays practised with model answers
Admin: Hall ticket, stationery, geometry box, scientific calculator (where permitted) — all ready

Exam Strategy — Kerala DHSE Specific Tips

  • 1Kerala DHSE rewards structured answers. Long-answer questions (5 marks) are evaluated with a specific answer scheme. Write answers in the same logical order as the textbook presents concepts — the evaluator is cross-checking against a fixed key. Structured, sequential answers score higher than verbose but disorganised ones.
  • 2Diagrams in Biology are mark-generators, not decorations. A well-labelled diagram in a 3-mark Biology question typically earns 1 full mark even if the written explanation is incomplete. Never write a Biology long answer without at least one diagram. Key diagrams: reproductive organs, DNA replication, lac operon, neuron, ecosystem pyramid, pollen grain TS.
  • 3Show every step in Physics and Chemistry numericals. DHSE uses step-marking for numerical problems — each correct formula, substitution, and unit gets partial marks even if the final answer is wrong. A careless student who skips steps and gets the right answer can score less than a careful student who shows all steps and makes one arithmetic error.
  • 4Use the reading time wisely. Each paper has 15 minutes of reading time before you can write. Read every question. Identify which 5-mark questions you are strongest in and plan your sequence. In Maths, scan for choice questions and decide which alternative to attempt before picking up your pen.
  • 5Previous year papers are your most important resource. The Kerala DHSE question pattern is highly consistent year to year. Questions on specific topics (Kirchhoff's laws, Nernst equation, Mendelian genetics, integration by parts) appear repeatedly in different forms. Solving 5 years of papers tells you exactly what is guaranteed to come.
  • 6Do not neglect English and score free marks. English Core in HSE is one of the most scoring papers — the reading comprehension, letter writing and grammar sections are highly predictable in format. Students who practise format-based questions for 30 minutes a week from October onwards regularly score 75+ out of 80 in the theory paper. Do not leave English preparation for the last week.
