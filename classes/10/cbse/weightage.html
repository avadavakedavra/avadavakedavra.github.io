CBSE Class 10 · Board Exam Guide 2025–26

CBSE Class 10 Chapter-Wise
Weightage 2025–26

A complete, subject-wise breakdown of marks distribution for the CBSE Class 10 board examinations. Know which chapters carry the most marks and where to focus your preparation.

Mathematics Science Social Science English
By SEHR Academy · Vadanappally, Thrissur · Updated May 2026

Every mark counts in the CBSE Class 10 board exam — and the students who score 90+ are not necessarily the ones who studied the most. They are the ones who studied the right chapters, the right amount. CBSE assigns different weightage to different units and chapters across all subjects, and knowing this distribution is the single most useful piece of exam strategy you can have. This guide gives you the official unit-wise and estimated chapter-wise marks breakdown for all core subjects in the 2025–26 academic year.

Important note: CBSE publishes official unit-wise weightage but does not release a formal chapter-wise breakdown. The chapter-level distribution in this guide is based on the official syllabus, CBSE sample papers, and question paper trends from the past five years. Unit-wise marks are authoritative; chapter-wise marks are best estimates for planning purposes.

How CBSE Class 10 Board Exams Work

Each CBSE Class 10 subject is assessed for a total of 100 marks, split into 80 marks for the written theory exam and 20 marks for internal assessment. The theory paper is held once a year (single annual examination format since 2023–24), and there is no separate term structure anymore.

The question paper follows a standard format with five sections: Section A for objective questions (MCQs and assertion–reason), Sections B, C and D for short and long answer questions, and Section E for case/source-based questions. Internal choices are provided in about one-third of the questions.

The internal assessment 20 marks are divided across: periodic tests (10 marks), multiple assessments such as quizzes and assignments (5 marks), subject enrichment activities (3 marks), and portfolio (2 marks). These marks are entirely within the school's control, making them an important low-effort scoring opportunity.

Mathematics — Chapter-Wise Weightage

Mathematics is available at two levels: Standard (Code 041) and Basic (Code 241). Both follow the same syllabus and chapter weightage; the difference lies in the difficulty level of questions. Algebra is the highest-scoring unit at 20 marks, followed by Geometry (15 marks) and Trigonometry (12 marks). Together these three units account for nearly 60% of the entire paper.

Science — Chapter-Wise Weightage

Science integrates Physics, Chemistry and Biology into a single paper. Chemical Substances and World of Living are the two biggest units, each worth 25 marks. Together they make up more than 60% of the paper. Light (Physics) and Electricity are also high-priority chapters. Natural Resources is the lowest-scoring unit with just 5 marks.

Social Science — Chapter-Wise Weightage

Social Science covers four subjects — History, Geography, Political Science (Civics) and Economics — each carrying equal weight. Each subject is worth 20 marks in the theory paper. The paper tends to be scoring because of its straightforward factual and conceptual nature, particularly in Economics and Political Science.

English — Paper Structure and Weightage

English does not follow a chapter-wise system in the same way as other subjects. The 80-mark paper is divided across three skill areas: Reading, Writing and Grammar, and Literature. The literature section carries the highest weight.

How to Use Weightage for Smart Preparation

  • 1Start with high-weightage units. In Maths, open with Algebra and Geometry. In Science, begin with Chemical Substances and World of Living. These units have the deepest question pools and require the most practice time.
  • 2Do not skip low-weightage chapters entirely. Natural Resources (5 marks in Science) and Number Systems (6 marks in Maths) are short chapters that can be completed in one or two sessions. Ignoring them completely means leaving easy marks on the table.
  • 3Secure your internal assessment 20 marks. These 20 marks are entirely school-based and predictable. Submit notebooks on time, take periodic tests seriously and complete all projects. This is the most reliable 20 marks in the entire 100-mark structure.
  • 4Practice CBSE sample papers. The official CBSE sample paper released each year is the closest proxy to the actual exam. Work through at least three years of sample papers under timed conditions before the board exam.
  • 5Target 40% competency-based questions. CBSE has raised the share of application-based and case-study questions to 40% of the paper. Rote-learning chapter content is no longer sufficient — you need to be able to apply concepts to new situations.
  • 6Practise map work in Social Science. 5 map-based marks are completely formula-driven — locate the correct places and you score full marks. Practice mapping at least 15–20 minutes a week from October onwards.
